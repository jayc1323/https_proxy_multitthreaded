      <h2>Course Summary</h2>

      <div>Welcome to CS 40 - Machine Structure and Assembly Language Programming! CS 40 will give you two important experiences:
        <ol style="margin:12px 0px !important;">

          <li>
          You will turn the corner from introductory programming to 
          programming as a professional.
          </li>

          <li>
          You will study in detail the structure of modern computers, and
          that in turn will add greatly to your understanding of how computer
          hardware, operating systems, compilers, language runtimes, and other
          tools work together to influence the performance of your programs.
          </li>

        </ol>
      
        These two goals are very closely related: all good computer professionals
        know how to apply deep knowledge of computing systems to their programming
        projects and to their research in computer science. Whether you are a
        newly confirmed CS major, someone who is considering a CS major but is
        unsure, or a student from another field who is interested in a deep look
        at some of the most important technologies of our time, CS 40 should
        give you an intense, realistic and (we hope!) exciting look at how modern
        computers work.

      </div>

      <p>This course is intended as an introduction to the theory
                of computation for senior level undergraduates and
                graduate students looking for background material
                in theory. The major topics within the course
                include: models of computation, finite automata,
                regular languages, context free grammars, turing machines,
                undecidability, infeasibility, diagonalizations, nondeterminism,
                information theory, time vs space, and complexity classes.</p>

      <p><b>Prerequisites:</b> MATH/COMP 61 and COMP 15 are recommended, but any prior course with rigorous mathematical proofs should suffice</p>
      
      <p><b>Primary Textbook: </b><a target="_blank" href="https://www.amazon.com/Introduction-Theory-Computation-Sipser/dp/8131525295/ref=sr_1_1?crid=25SOINE160A9I&keywords=Introduction+to+the+Theory+of+Computation+by+Michael+Sipser&qid=1657724171&s=books&sprefix=introduction+to+the+theory+of+computation+by+michael+sipser%2Cstripbooks%2C67&sr=1-1"><i>Introduction to the Theory of Computation</i></a> by Michael Sipser

      <p>This course is intended as an introduction to the theory
                of computation for senior level undergraduates and
                graduate students looking for background material
                in theory. The major topics within the course
                include: models of computation, finite automata,
                regular languages, context free grammars, turing machines,
                undecidability, infeasibility, diagonalizations, nondeterminism,
                information theory, time vs space, and complexity classes.</p>

      <p><b>Prerequisites:</b> MATH/COMP 61 and COMP 15 are recommended, but any prior course with rigorous mathematical proofs should suffice</p>
      
      <p><b>Primary Textbook: </b><a target="_blank" href="https://www.amazon.com/Introduction-Theory-Computation-Sipser/dp/8131525295/ref=sr_1_1?crid=25SOINE160A9I&keywords=Introduction+to+the+Theory+of+Computation+by+Michael+Sipser&qid=1657724171&s=books&sprefix=introduction+to+the+theory+of+computation+by+michael+sipser%2Cstripbooks%2C67&sr=1-1"><i>Introduction to the Theory of Computation</i></a> by Michael Sipser

      <p>This course is intended as an introduction to the theory
                of computation for senior level undergraduates and
                graduate students looking for background material
                in theory. The major topics within the course
                include: models of computation, finite automata,
                regular languages, context free grammars, turing machines,
                undecidability, infeasibility, diagonalizations, nondeterminism,
                information theory, time vs space, and complexity classes.</p>
